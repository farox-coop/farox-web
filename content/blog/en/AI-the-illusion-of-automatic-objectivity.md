For centuries, knowledge was tied to human experience, a process that involved thinking, processing information, but also discussion, interpretation and correction.
</br></br>
Today, that chain is undergoing a series of transformations that lead us to ask: **Are more data and greater speed synonymous with better information?**
</br></br>
In this new landscape, an increasing number of artificial intelligence systems are trained on content generated by other artificial intelligences. Texts, images, summaries, analyses, code. Information produced by models that were themselves trained on prior information.
</br></br>
The result is a quiet yet significant phenomenon: synthetic knowledge feeding new synthetic knowledge.
</br></br>
The question is no longer merely technical, it is epistemological: what happens when knowledge begins to detach from the human experience that originally gave rise to it?

## When AI trains AI

In recent years, various studies have begun warning about a risk that until recently seemed theoretical: the progressive deterioration of knowledge quality when models are trained predominantly on data generated by other models.
</br></br>
The process is roughly as follows:  
* A model is trained on human-produced content.  
* It generates new content.  
* That content circulates online.  
* Other models incorporate it as training material.

And the cycle continues.
</br></br>
The problem is not that AI generates content, but what happens when that output begins to displace the original source. In this context, troubling effects begin to emerge from the loss of conceptual diversity to the simplification of complex ideas, the reinforcement of existing biases, and the amplification of errors.
</br></br>
In short, as Palahniuk might say, **everything becomes "a copy of a copy of a copy."** **Each iteration retains something of the original, but loses nuance.** Anyone who has played the telephone game understands this perfectly.
</br></br>
The deeper issue is that, over time, this degradation becomes invisible. The risk is not that AI "gets things wrong." The risk is that it begins constructing a version of the world that is increasingly homogeneous, predictable, and disconnected from lived experience.
</br></br>
Up to this point, the problem might seem to lie in the data. But it doesn’t end there.

## The Algorithm is not a Judge – It is a Policy

There is a seductive and dangerously convenient idea that surfaces whenever a sensitive decision is automated: "the machine has no interests." As if the absence of emotions implied the absence of bias.
</br></br>
But an algorithm is not an impartial judge. **An algorithm is a system designed to achieve a goal.** And that goal is defined by someone: a company, a team, a state, a provider, an individual.
</br></br>
Even if the dataset were flawless, the algorithm continues making decisions at every step:

* what it prioritizes  
* what it considers "anomalous"  
* what it interprets as risk  
* what it labels as truth

In this sense, speaking of "automatic objectivity" confuses two different things: consistency with fairness. A decision can be consistent and still be unjust. An algorithm can be coherent and still reinforce an agenda. Because non-neutrality does not reside only in the data, it is embedded in the design.

## Two Layers of the Problem: Data and Algorithm

When discussing "quality" in AI, the focus is often placed almost exclusively on data: its volume, diversity, origin, freshness. And that makes sense, a model learns from the material it is trained on.
</br></br>
But there is another layer, more uncomfortable because it is less visible, that also shapes reality: the algorithm as a set of decisions. What is penalized? What is considered "success"? What is discarded as noise?
</br></br>
In other words, even with "good data", the way it is processed is never neutral.
</br></br>
When degraded data is combined with algorithms presented as objective, a perfect storm emerges: **systems that appear trustworthy while constructing a version of the world that becomes increasingly difficult to audit.**

## Diella and the Anti-Corruption Promise

A striking example that makes this impossible to ignore is the case of Diella, an AI formally appointed as "Minister of Public Procurement" in Albania.

![Diella IA Albania](/images/blog/AI-the-illusion-of-automatic-objectivity-diella.png)

The narrative is tempting: gradually transferring public tender decisions to an automated system to "reduce political influence", advance anti-corruption reforms, and align with European Union accession requirements.
</br></br>
Who wouldn’t want that?
</br></br>
Yet here lies the blind spot: assuming that Artificial Intelligence is synonymous with "incorruptible" is overlooking a basic truth.
</br></br>
If those who design the system have corrupt incentives, or if those who define the awarding criteria are corrupt, then AI does not eliminate corruption, it formalizes it. It makes it less debatable, harder to appeal, and easier to legitimize.
</br></br>
In that sense, **the great trick of automation is not merely executing decisions, it is cloaking them in neutrality.** And in contexts of power, that neutrality can be more dangerous than explicit bias, because it ceases to be visible and debatable. It ceases to be human.

## Knowledge as a Human Process

Human knowledge has never been neutral. It is shaped by cultures, contexts, debates, conflicts, and corrections. That is precisely why it is valuable: because it is auditable, debatable, revisable.
</br></br>
Wikipedia became one of the most important sources for training language models precisely for this reason. It is imperfect, but transparent. It has edit histories, debates, references, collective moderation. It is not a perfect system, it is a human system. And its strength lies in that condition.
</br></br>
In fact, when a collaborative platform is replaced by an automated system, what is lost is not only human participation, it is traceability, and with it, a fundamental component of trust.
</br></br>
In other words, **objectivity does not arise from the absence of people, it emerges from dialogue among many people**.

## The Myth of Algorithmic Objectivity

At the same time, projects like Grokipedia, promoted within Elon Musk’s ecosystem as an alternative to Wikipedia, present themselves as less biased, faster, and more precise. An automated platform that promises to offer "more objective" versions of knowledge.

![Grokipedia IA](/images/blog/AI-the-illusion-of-automatic-objectivity-grokipedia.png)

But that promise raises new questions: Who decides what information is valid? Who corrects errors? How –and by whom– is the process audited?
</br></br>
This reveals an increasingly unavoidable tension: while Wikipedia remains one of the primary training sources for large models due to its open and relatively neutral nature, it is simultaneously targeted by public attacks from actors with explicit interests in undermining its legitimacy.
</br></br>
In this context, **replacing imperfect yet auditable human sources with opaque systems that present themselves as neutral does not seem like progress.** It may, however, represent a paradigm shift.

## The Role of Judgment

From a technical perspective, the challenge is quite clear: improve datasets, design validation mechanisms, diversify sources. But the problem is not only technical, it is cultural.
</br></br>
The development of generative AI forces us to bring back to the center a word that technological enthusiasm often sidelines: judgment.

- Judgment in the data we use.
- Judgment in the sources we prioritize.
- Judgment in validation processes.
- Judgment in the objectives encoded as rules.

Because automation does not eliminate human decisions, it relocates them to a less visible place. **This is not about distrusting technology. It is about understanding that technology does not replace human responsibility, even if it displaces it.**
</br></br>
If knowledge begins to be constructed primarily from closed, automated and self-referential systems, what is at stake is not only the quality of information. It is the way we understand the world.
</br></br>
And if systems that promise "objectivity" teach us anything, it is this: there is nothing more human –and more political– than deciding what counts as truth.
