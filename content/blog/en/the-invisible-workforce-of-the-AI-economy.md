Over the past decade, a quiet transformation has been unfolding in the background of our digital lives.
</br></br>
Every photo we take.  
Every place we scan.  
Every route we walk.  
Every object a camera recognizes.

## All of it Becomes Data

Most of the time we experience these interactions simply as part of using a product: a game, a social network, a mapping service, a recommendation system.
</br></br>
But if we look at the process from another perspective, something else is happening. Millions of people are continuously generating structured information about the physical and digital world: streets, buildings, landmarks, objects, routes, habits, preferences and patterns of movement. **And that information is becoming the raw material for new industries.**
</br></br>
There is a structural problem in this dynamic that often goes unnoticed: most people have no real way of knowing how the data they generate while using an application or digital service will ultimately be used.
</br></br>
In theory, that information is contained in the terms and conditions. In practice, these are long, complex documents that are nearly impossible to read in full.
</br></br>
That formal consent may be sufficient from a legal standpoint, but it leaves open a deeper question: **to what extent do we actually understand what kinds of infrastructures are being built from our digital activity?**
</br></br>
At the same time, regulation moves more slowly than technology. While governments around the world have begun discussing frameworks to protect data and privacy, we are still far from having global policies capable of balancing power between platforms, users and digital infrastructures.

## An Interesting Example

When **Pokemon Go** launched in 2016, it was introduced as an augmented reality game.
</br></br>
Players walked through cities, parks and landmarks with their phones to capture creatures that appeared overlaid onto the real world.

![Pokemon Go](/images/blog/the-invisible-workforce-of-the-AI-economy-pokemon-go.png)

Over time, the game introduced features that allowed users to scan their surroundings with the phone camera to improve positioning and the augmented reality experience.
</br></br>
As time went on, those scans accumulated into billions of images of the physical world. Later, that material was used to build visual positioning systems — navigation systems that complement GPS with visual references from the environment.
</br></br>
These types of systems are extremely valuable because they allow robots to navigate cities, improve autonomous systems and enable machines to understand physical space with greater precision.
</br></br>
In other words, **what began as a game ended up becoming a massive dataset of the world.** In short, a business.

## The Pattern is not Exceptional

Pokemon Go is not an anomaly. If anything, it is a particularly visible example of a much broader logic.
</br></br>
**Google Maps** uses aggregated and anonymized data from users who have location history enabled to estimate traffic conditions, peak hours, waiting times and mobility patterns. **Waze**, in turn, relies heavily on reports from its community about accidents, speed cameras, road closures and other events to optimize routes and generate useful information for drivers.
</br></br>
**Tesla** claims to use billions of miles of real-world driving data to train its assisted driving systems, and its privacy policy explains that certain videos used for fleet learning are only shared with explicit data-sharing consent. In other words, even when consent exists, the structural principle remains the same: **everyday product usage generates data that strengthens a highly valuable technological infrastructure**.
</br></br>
Every time we use a map, report an incident, tag an image or interact with an automated system, we are not just consuming a tool — we are also contributing to improving it.
</br></br>
The pattern is quite consistent: ordinary user activity generates structured data that is aggregated at massive scale and eventually becomes infrastructure.

## The Invisible Labor

This raises an interesting question:
</br>
Who actually produced that data?
</br></br>
The answer is simple: the users. Millions of them.
</br></br>
People who walked through cities, pointed their cameras at buildings, reported traffic incidents, drove cars, took photos or simply moved around with an app open.
</br></br>
They were not employees.  
They were not contractors.  
They were not data labelers.
</br></br>
They were simply using a product.
</br></br>
And yet the data generated through those interactions ended up becoming the foundation for technologies used in logistics, robotics, mapping systems, predictive models and AI training datasets.
</br></br>
**In many ways, this resembles a new form of invisible labor.**
</br></br>
Not labor in the traditional sense, but the continuous production of valuable information through everyday digital behavior. Sometimes this is referred to as data exhaust — the byproduct of using digital systems — but calling it "exhaust" can be misleading, because in many cases that "residue" is actually the most valuable part of the system.

## The Asymmetric Exchange of the Data Economy

If we think about this phenomenon as a new form of work, another question naturally follows: what does the user receive in return for their contribution?
</br></br>
It would be inaccurate to say we receive nothing. In exchange for our data we are given:

* Free navigation  
* Entertainment  
* More relevant recommendations  
* Access to digital infrastructures that would otherwise be extremely costly to maintain

This is the implicit trade behind much of the digital economy: the company offers a useful service and the user generates data that, in some cases, genuinely improves the experience. **The problem is not that this exchange exists, the problem is its asymmetry.**
</br></br>
Value is not distributed evenly. The user gains convenience, personalization or entertainment, while the platform operator gains strategic assets.
</br></br>
And those assets are far from trivial: models, maps, navigation systems, computer vision infrastructure, recommendation engines and competitive advantages that are difficult to replicate.
</br></br>
In other words, **we — the users — are paid in convenience, while companies capitalize at scale.**
</br></br>
Platforms collect the data.  
Companies build infrastructure on top of it.  
And entire industries emerge around that infrastructure.

## The Deeper Issue: Artificial Intelligence

This dynamic reveals something important about the digital economy: **the most valuable resource is no longer software alone, it is the information generated by human behavior.**
</br></br>
Data about the world.  
Data about how we move through it.  
Data about what we see, photograph or search for.
</br></br>
**Many AI systems ultimately depend on this collective flow of human activity**, which leads to a question that is becoming increasingly difficult to ignore: who should control the infrastructure built on top of that data?
</br></br>
Here a growing tension emerges in today's technology ecosystem. **Every day we interact with systems that we cannot inspect, audit or control.** In other words, we generate the data that feeds them, we depend on their results, yet we have very little visibility into how they actually work.
</br></br>
This creates a structural imbalance: the intelligence of these systems depends on the participation of millions of people, but the infrastructure that sustains them is concentrated in a very small number of platforms.
</br></br>
Which brings us back to the beginning of this story. The issue is no longer simply how useful a tool is, or how entertaining a game might be, but also who captures the value that these systems generate.
</br></br>
A few months ago, **Raffi Krikorian**, CTO of Mozilla, spoke at the DLD Conference about how much of today's AI infrastructure is being built on platforms we do not control. He argued for the development of open alternatives across different layers of the AI stack, not necessarily to replace large technology companies, but to help rebalance technological power.

[Raffi Krikorian at DLD Conference](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xCckoDNxc8c)

## An Alternative Direction

The people who generate this information rarely control the systems built from it. This is one of the reasons why conversations around **self-hosted AI** are gaining traction.
</br></br>
Self-hosted systems do not eliminate all challenges, but they do change something fundamental: the relationship between data, infrastructure and power.
</br></br>
Instead of sending information to centralized platforms, organizations and individuals can run models locally, define how their data is processed and decide what is stored, what is shared and for what purposes.
</br></br>
While this clearly does not represent a direct payment to users for the data they generate, it does reduce extraction and restore a degree of control. And that matters, because part of the problem we face today goes beyond economics, it is also political.
</br></br>
Who hosts the intelligence.  
Who defines the rules.  
Who audits the processes.  
Who benefits from the infrastructure built from our activity.

## Conscious Users

We need to keep talking about these issues to foster more conscious users, more sovereign technologies and more balanced digital structures.
</br></br>
This does not mean we should stop playing Pokemon Go or avoid using Waze the next time we want to figure out the fastest bike route to a friend's house. Bringing these issues to the table invites us to ask questions about the platforms we use, about the content of the Terms and Conditions we accept, and to demand that public institutions regulate how our data is used and protect our privacy.
</br></br>
In short, it invites us to make better decisions.
</br></br>
The next stage of the AI conversation will likely not revolve around larger models or faster systems, but around something more immediate:

* Who owns the intelligence generated by human activity?  
* And who controls the infrastructure built from it?

Because if the past decade has taught us anything, it is that the most valuable asset of the digital economy is not code.
</br></br>
**It's us.**
